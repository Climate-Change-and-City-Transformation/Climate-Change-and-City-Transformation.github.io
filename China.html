Depth into Carbon Dioxide Emission in China

By Xudong Zhang

Our climate has been affected deeply by the emission of carbon dioxide for a very long time. Recent years, China has become one of the largest carbon dioxide emission countries . In 2017, it released 9.8 billion tons carbon dioxide. Most of China's carbon dioxide emissions come from fossil fuels, esopecially from the coal (70% of total energy comes from coal).

To look deep into the relationships among carbon dioxide emission, economic structures, geographical elements and population in China, we first need to understand current emission pattern at the level of province or city, and how it transformed from 2000 to 2016 (when the development of economy in China was relatively higher than any period since 1978). This analysis could give us a initial but relatively detailed cognition of where contributed more emission than other places.

So, the first question is: Where are carbon dioxide emissions higher than the average in China?

The Carbon Dioxide Emission Map of China, 2017
Introduction to distribution map

In this example, all the original emission data was divided by the average emission volume. This means that, on the map, the regions emitting more carbon dioxide than average(above 1) are shaded in pink and less emitting regions (below 1)are coloured by green. You can move your mouse over each area to see the specific emission ratio.

Province Map: Clearly Spatial Agglomeration

In China, the most developed regions and cities are located in the south or east of China. From this province boundary map, the more developed provinces, such as Guangdong province, Shanghai, produced relatively more carbon dioxide annually. But some provinces(Inner Mongolia, Shanxi, Heibei,) that located in the North of China, still contain much higher emission level, compared to the average level.

City Map: Agglomeration Mixed with Sporadic Distribution

This is cities' boundary map of China. It was coloured by the ratio data, like the province map.It gives us more clear view of which cities contribute more carbon dioxide than others. The cities that emit more carbon dioxide than the average level aggregated in the North and Eastern coastal region of China. Some core cities(the cities that contain the circles, these cities usually have more access to various resources in the provinces to which they belong) make more contribution to emission, compared to the other cities in the same provinces relatively, and a sporadic distribution pattern emerged.

Scatter Chart: Where are spatial elements in Data World?

The animation combined the shape transformation of cities, provinces and their core cities into one chart.The X axis is the the ratio of carbon dioxide emission. The Y axis is province.The shapes in the chart is positioned by their ratio of carbon dioxide emission. The small, coloured circles denote the cities, and circles without colour are core cities.The bigger are provinces. As we can see, most of the core cities distributed at the right part of the line, which means that these cities emitted more carbon dioxide than others. 9 provinces (the bigger circles) are above the average level. Among them, the North provinces cover 66.7% of total(Shandong, Hebei, Inner Mongolia, Henan, Liaoning, Shanxi).

Transformation of CO2 Emission in 50 Cities

The carbon dioxide emission pattern has been displayed above. Next, we want to know how this pattern came into being.Based on the above analysis, we know that core cities in China made a more contribution to the emission of carbon dioxide.Also because the original, well-prepared transformation dataset of China cities' carbon emission from Haikun Wang etc we used focuses on 50 cities, and most of them are the core cities of China, it is appropriate for us to explore the changes of cities in the context of carbon dioxide emission.

For the rest of the part, we will introduce time line (2000-2016) into our analysis and explore how the emission volume grew and what sectors contributed more emissions in each city.This could help us understand what kinds of development path the cities choosed during this period.

Here the first question is: How did carbon dioxide emissions of cities grow from 2000 to 2016 in China?

The map below uses the annual volume of carbon dioxide emission in each city as the variable of the white circles. As time went by, the most noticeable regions were the East part (e.g. Yangtze Delta),South part (Pearl River Delta) and North part (Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei) of China. If you click on the circles in the map, you might find that most of cities in these three areas emitted more carbon dioxide than that in any other cities in each year. Although some cities may have similiar volume of carbon dioxide emissions, their structures of emission may have sighnificant differences. Next graph could give us more information about the transformation of economic structures of emission in each city.

This graph shows us the structure of emission in each city. We choose primary industry, construction, transportation, commercial, residents, and other industry as the analysis objects. The changes of ratio of emission volume among sectors in each city are relatively small, most of cities followed their previous path to develop. But we still could find some differences if we make a comparison between cities. Taking Shenzhen and Guangzhou as an example, both of them are the largest cities in China. But their emission structure is different. The commercial sector in Guangzhou covered a large percentage of emission annually. But Shenzhen is relatively more balanced.

So far, we have already talked about the spatial pattern of carbon dioxide emission and the transformation of 50 cities' emission sectors. Next, we will combine the population, GDP, region information, carbon dioxide emission data together, to see what kind of division of cities came into being as time went by.

So, the last question: Is their any geographical differences among China cities when considering the relationships between GDP and emission volume?

North/South Divide

If you’re familiar with the geography and climates of China, you might know that the cities which are at the North of Qinling-Huaihe Line, will be regarded as the North cities. Most of North cities uses coal as major energy to heat and generate the electricity. Some heavy industries, such as Metal smelter, also prefer to distributing in the North, because of the geographical proximity to natural resources. These reasons resulted in more emission in North China.

We still focus on the 50 cities we mentioned before.The above graph shows the distribution of cities in the GDP-Total Carbon dioxide Emission context. The division pattern of cities emerged as time went by. In 2016, there were 10 North cities above the trend line, covering more than 70% of total cities which were above this line. All of cities in the South of China were below the trend line, and the majority of cities from East and West were same as South cities. This distribution means that South cities could produce more economic outputs, compared with North cities, under the same carbon dioxide emission. This graph clearly shows when the division between South and North is explicit and what kind of pattern it is.

What is next?

In this section, we mainly focus on the carbon dioxide emission topic in China, where the emission has already become the largest one. Carbon dioxide emission has affected almost all aspects of our life and environment, such as temperature, sea level, and the distribution of species. It is crucial for us to continue working on analysis of the current emission spatial pattern, future trends and its impacts.
