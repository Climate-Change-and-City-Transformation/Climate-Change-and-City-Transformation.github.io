Depth into Carbon Dioxide Emission in China

By Xudong Zhang

China is one of the largest carbon dioxide emission countries. In 2017, it released 9.8 billion tons carbon dioxide. Most of China's carbon dioxide emissions come from fossil fuels, esopecially from the coal (70% of total energy comes from coal).

To look deep into the relationships among carbon dioxide emission, economic structures, geographical elements and population in China, we first need to understand current emission pattern at the level of province or city, and how it transformed from 2000 to 2016 (when the development of economy in China was relatively higher than any period since 1978). This analysis could give us a initial but relatively detailed cognition of where contributed more emission than other places.

So, the first question is: Where are carbon dioxide emissions higher than the average in China?

The Carbon Dioxide Emission Map of China, 2017
Introduction to distribution map

In this example, all the original emission data was divided by the average emission volume. This means that, on the map, the regions emitting more carbon dioxide than average(above 1) are shaded in pink and less emitting regions (below 1)are coloured by green. You can move your mouse over each area to see the specific emission ratio.

Province Map: Clearly Spatial Agglomeration

In China, the most developed regions and cities are located in the south or east of China. From this province boundary map, the more developed provinces, such as Guangdong province, Shanghai, produced relatively more carbon dioxide annually. But some provinces(Inner Mongolia, Shanxi, Heibei,) that located in the North of China, still contain much higher emission level, compared to the average level.

City Map: Agglomeration Mixed with Sporadic Distribution

This is cities' boundary map of China. It was coloured by the ratio data, like the province map.It gives us more clear view of which cities contribute more carbon dioxide than others. The cities that emit more carbon dioxide than the average level aggregated in the North and Eastern coastal region of China. Some core cities(the cities that contain the circles, these cities usually have more access to various resources in the provinces to which they belong) make more contribution to emission, compared to the other cities in the same provinces relatively, and a sporadic distribution pattern emerged.

Scatter Chart: Where are spatial elements in Data World?

The animation combined the shape transformation of cities, provinces and their core cities into one chart.The X axis is the the ratio of carbon dioxide emission. The Y axis is province.The shapes in the chart is positioned by their ratio of carbon dioxide emission. The small, coloured circles denote the cities, and circles without colour are core cities.The bigger are provinces. As we can see, most of the core cities distributed at the right part of the line, which means that these cities emitted more carbon dioxide than others. 9 provinces (the bigger circles) are above the average level. Among them, the North provinces cover 66.7% of total(Shandong, Hebei, Inner Mongolia, Henan, Liaoning, Shanxi).
